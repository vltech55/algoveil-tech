Why hybrid retrieval

Dense embeddings capture semantic similarity but miss exact-string matches — the query "section 4.2 policy override" retrieves the correct section by BM25, not by cosine similarity. Sparse-only retrieval misses paraphrase — the query "what triggers a manual review" retrieves a section titled "human-in-the-loop escalation" by embedding, not by BM25.

Production RAG needs both. Acuity runs both in parallel and fuses the ranked lists with reciprocal rank fusion (RRF) using the standard k = 60 constant. RRF is order-of-magnitude cheaper than a learned fusion model and is remarkably robust — it beats either single retriever on every eval slice we've measured.

Each retriever returns the top-N documents ranked by its own scoring function. RRF fuses them into a single ranked list using the formula:

score(d) = Σ 1 / (k + rank_i(d)) where k = 60 and rank_i(d) is the position of document d in the i-th retriever's ranked list (or ∞ if not returned).

The constant k = 60 comes from Cormack et al. and is a well-defended default — Acuity leaves it configurable but ships with 60.

Rerank & RRF

RRF gives us a shortlist. A cross-encoder then rescores the top 20 candidates against the original query with a bi-directional attention model — this is expensive (one model call per candidate) but the shortlist is small and the quality lift is substantial. The final top-5 goes to the LLM as the retrieval context.

Citation faithfulness

Every claim in the LLM's answer is tagged with a citation to the retrieved chunk it derives from. Faithfulness is scored per-claim: does the retrieved chunk actually support the claim?

Acuity uses a two-pass approach:

  • Generation produces answers with inline citations of the form [chunk_id].
  • A separate model call scores each claim/chunk pair on the RAGAS faithfulness rubric.
  • Claims below the threshold are flagged; the answer is returned with a "reduced confidence" indicator rather than silently kept.
The hard part isn't retrieval quality. It's proving retrieval quality — and catching regressions before they ship to users. — From the design note

Eval harness

A golden-set of question/answer/expected-citation triples runs on every merge to main. The CI job:

  • Re-runs the full pipeline against the golden set.
  • Computes precision@k, recall@k, RRF-fused NDCG, and per-claim faithfulness.
  • Emits a JSON report keyed to the git SHA.
  • Fails the build if any metric regresses more than ε below the last main commit.

Every prompt change becomes measurable. Every eval failure has a specific claim/chunk pair to inspect.

Streaming

The LLM streams tokens back to the caller starting from the first sentence-boundary — the client sees a partial answer within ~800ms of the query, even though the full grounded answer takes 2–3 seconds. Citations attach to claims incrementally as they arrive.

When to use it

Acuity is the right starting point when: (a) you need answers grounded in a specific corpus (docs, contracts, policies, internal wiki), (b) the corpus is more than ~1,000 documents so a single-shot LLM context doesn't work, and (c) you need to prove answer quality with an audit trail — not just ship and hope.

It is not the right fit for: (a) open-domain chat without a specific corpus, (b) sub-second interactive UIs where the two-pass faithfulness scoring is too slow, or (c) cases where hallucination is fine (creative writing, brainstorming).
